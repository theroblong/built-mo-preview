Macro signals that move CPG demand
FRED hosts 800,000+ economic time series. These four are the highest-signal inputs for CPG demand forecasting — each maps directly to consumer behavior at the shelf and is incorporated into the Aevah demand model.
How macro signals enter the forecast
How macro signals flow into the Aevah forecast
FRED → Aevah Data Layer → Mo Demand Forecast
Same data architecture as SPINS — a different upstream source feeding the same model
1
Pull from FRED API
Weekly cron hits FRED REST API for 5 series. Free API key. No rate limits for standard use.
2
Normalize to weekly grain
Gas prices are already weekly. Monthly series (sentiment, unemployment) are forward-filled to weekly to match SPINS cadence.
3
Lag-feature engineering
Consumer behavior lags macro events. Feature set includes 4-week and 8-week lags — captures the delay between economic signal and shelf behavior.
4
Join to Mo Forecast
Macro features join by week to the SKU × retailer training set. Mo learns which signals matter for each channel — C-store and grocery respond differently to the same macro event.
What we draw from FRED
Four economic signals incorporated into the Aevah demand model
GASDESW · Weekly
Retail Gas Prices
US avg, dollars per gallon · updated every Monday
CPG signal
↑ C-storeHigh gas prices → more frequent fill-ups → more impulse buys at C-store per fill-up trip
↓ GroceryHigh gas prices reduce discretionary grocery trips — fewer impulse purchases per visit
Strong weekly correlation; highest-frequency macro signal available
UMCSENT · Monthly
Consumer Sentiment Index
Univ. of Michigan · 0–100 scale · updated monthly
CPG signal
↓ PremiumLow sentiment → trade-down from premium to private label; ARP compression across shelf
↑ MultipackLow sentiment drives value-seeking behavior — larger pack sizes outperform singles
Consumer sentiment lags 6–8 weeks behind shelf behavior — planned feature in Mo
UNRATE · Monthly
Unemployment Rate
Bureau of Labor Statistics · seasonally adjusted
CPG signal
↑ Value packsRising unemployment drives pantry-loading behavior — 12pk and value-format outperform
↓ ImpulseFalling employment → confidence → more impulse single-unit purchasing at C-store
Interacts with cannibalization rate — value packs more incremental in high-unemployment environments
CPIAUCSL · Monthly
Consumer Price Index
Bureau of Labor Statistics · all urban consumers
CPG signal
± ElasticityHigh CPI shifts the elasticity curve — consumers become more price-sensitive; promo lift compresses at lower discount depths
↓ ARPSustained CPI pressure suppresses ARP gains — consumers resist price increases even in premium segments
Use YoY delta, not absolute level, as the feature
