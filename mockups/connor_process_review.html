2 · Pain Points (Direct from Transcript)
1
Promo lift assumptions are guesswork
Sales team lift estimates come from historical feel, broker guidance, or buyer conversation — not modeled from data. Environmental factors (competitor on promo same week, recency of prior promo) compound the error and are invisible to Connor's model.
"I don't think they always have the greatest lift assumptions on their promotions. There's definitely a lot of room for improvement on forecasting these lifts." — Connor
"Was there another brand promoted at the same time? Was there a competitor promoted immediately before us? How many weeks prior did we last promote? Those are some things that can throw off the lift too." — Brian
2
100 Excel planners opened manually each month
Each of 6–7 sales reps maintains 10–20 retailer planner files. Connor opens every one, extracts promo data, and copies it into the master file. Not automated. Reps often haven't updated because they're traveling. Version control prevents shared access.
"I open up 100 of those Excel files, bring some of that data in pretty manually. It's not very efficient." — Connor
3
New SKU / cold-start ramp prediction is a guess
When a new flavor launches at a retailer with no history, the team defaults to sales team estimates and eyeballs how "other items performed." The ramp-up curve is assumed to max out at 12 weeks — but it varies significantly by retailer and format.
"Where we're adding a SKU and we don't have this historical data, we default to the sales team estimate... we have a lot of manual overrides where we don't have data or we're like guessing what the steady state is." — Connor
4
Data-dark retailers (Winco, HEB, TJ's) — flying blind
Major volume retailers with zero category visibility. Winco is the most problematic: high volatility, competitor assortment changing constantly, and no data to detect the shift. Forecast assumptions go stale quickly and corrections come too late.
"Winco is probably the number one retailer where we struggle the most with forecasting... we don't act quickly enough because it's just hard to — this is one of 100 customers." — Connor
5
No new distribution signal in the model
If BUILT is adding 500 stores in October for a new flavor, Connor has no data-driven way to project the ramp. All store expansion knowledge lives only in the sales team's heads and planners.
"How would it predict the forecast without pulling another data source on, hey, we're going to add this flavor in October in 500 stores? Do you just have to pull from another source then to kind of add to the forecast?" — Connor
6
Zero macro signal integration
No model driver for gas prices, consumer sentiment, unemployment, or any external demand factor. Connor manually monitors these (U of M index, etc.) but can't incorporate them into the forecast systematically. "Too hard to manually track."
"We don't have a macro like impact driver on this model right now because it's too hard for us to manually track, and AI would be super helpful in that area." — Connor
7
Scale has outgrown the process
Started at 20 customers, now 100+, continuously adding. Adding SKUs requires manual row insertions and formula maintenance. The file is "unwieldy." The process was built for a smaller business and hasn't been redesigned — it's been stretched.
"We've just gotten so big that this file is becoming just unwieldy to update every month. We're probably not doing really well at tracking just because of our scale and because of the amount of SKUs we're adding." — Connor
8
No independent sanity check on the forecast
Connor's model is the only forecast. There's no second model to compare against, no backtesting against actuals, no confidence scoring per retailer-SKU. When the model is wrong, they usually find out from NetSuite actuals after the fact.
"Could we just feed it last three years spins data and then last three years actual sales data from NetSuite, and ask it to draw a curve forward-looking... We just compare it against what this model is showing. That would be really helpful, just as another source to check this against." — Connor
9
Weekly S&OP meetings consume Connor all-day every Monday
Every Monday = 30-min session per sales rep × 6–7 reps + Connor + Director of Planning + VP Finance Jeff. The number gets flashed from Excel, then discussed. Jeff keeps it weekly because it holds the team accountable. Ambition is to go monthly but growth won't allow it yet.
"It's every Monday, typically all day, 30 minutes per salesperson... pretty much all day for myself, our director of planning, our VP in finance. So they're pretty time consuming." — Connor
10
No quantified competitive/category intelligence at scale
Brian's team runs a scorecard but doing it across 100 retailers × multiple geographies × pricing/promo/assortment/cannibalization dimensions is genuinely intractable manually. 5,000+ SKUs, 300+ competitors, 500+ brands.
"We look across all 100 retailers, it's a lot of big data to really see what's driving change and what we can learn." — Brian
11
Cannibalization is acknowledged but not modeled
Connor's model has a cannibalization column — but it's manually estimated. Neither the rate nor the donor-impact direction is computed from data. Connor and Brian both named it unprompted as a key signal they're missing.
"What one thing I'd be curious about... cannibalization prediction. Yeah, and like ramp up — how has the ramp performed at other retailers? We kind of just guess on that." — Connor & Brian
3 · Four-Lens Evaluation
1
What can / does Aevah do to help Connor right now?
  • Velocity extract (MO_70): Every SPINS cycle, a fresh retailer × SKU table of 52-week actuals, 13-week LightGBM forecast, and category benchmarks. This is exactly the "extract per SPINS update" Bracken mentioned. Connor can feed it directly into his base velocity column instead of doing the L12-week SPINS copy-paste manually.
  • Seasonality index: The 12-month demand index Aevah already computes from SPINS replaces the seasonality tab Connor manually maintains. Auto-refreshed each cycle.
  • Cannibalization rates: scored_cannibalization table has computed rates for ~48% of BUILT's SKU pairs. For any retailer-SKU where a new flavor is being added, Aevah can provide the expected cannibalization coefficient — replacing Connor's manual column.
  • Price elasticity scores: MO_16/17 price response by retailer-SKU. When promo involves a price reduction, elasticity translates the cut to a predicted unit lift — more rigorous than the sales team's gut estimate.
  • Proxy ramp reference: New SKU cold-start overlay (just demoed in meeting). Comparable past BUILT launches at the same channel and format give a data-grounded ramp trajectory vs. ETS uncertainty band.
  • Competitive intelligence (Mo Trends): Who's taking share, which competitors raised price, competitor promo timing. Connor said "super valuable" in the meeting.
2
Is it real? How does it specifically help Connor?

All core capabilities above are built on live SPINS data already ingested in Druid — not prototypes or mocks. The LightGBM models (MO_26 for velocity, MO_20 for cannibalization rate) are trained and scoring today. The velocity extract was delivered this morning with real SPINS figures. What's real, specific, and grounded:

  • Base velocity column: Connor currently copies L12-week SPINS manually. Our table gives him a LightGBM-calibrated q50 (plus q10/q90 band) — same data, model-improved, auto-updated. He keeps override control; Aevah provides the starting point.
  • Promo lift: MO_72 causal analysis measures actual lift from past promotions — not predicted by ML yet, but the historical lift-rate-by-retailer-SKU is computable now. The gap is: wrapping it into a query ("at Winco, 2-for-$6, what lift?") with Mo Chat. The underlying signal already exists.
  • Cannibalization column: Connor has a column; we have a score. The handoff is a CSV extract by retailer-SKU pair. This is a one-API-call integration.
  • Independent sanity check: Connor asked explicitly for "another source to check against." Our LightGBM 13-week forecast IS that second model. We can produce a side-by-side: Connor's model vs. Aevah's ML vs. NetSuite actuals.

The honest answer on data-dark retailers (Winco, HEB): We can't see their category data either. But we can proxy from SPINS retailers in the same channel/region. This is an inference, not a measurement — and Connor should know the difference. Framing it as a proxy confidence interval (not a prediction) maintains trust.

3
What additional data model inputs make this better and more scalable?
  • NetSuite actuals integration: The single most high-value data add. Connor already pulls this manually for historicals and MTD. If Aevah ingests NetSuite, we can: (a) auto-backtest Connor's forecast vs. actuals per retailer-SKU, (b) compute forecast bias by rep/account, (c) close the loop on model improvement.
  • Sales team planner digitization: The 100 Excel planners are the biggest manual bottleneck. Near-term: a lightweight structured input (a shared sheet or simple form) that reps fill in once — consumed by both Aevah and Connor's model. This eliminates the 100-file open cycle entirely.
  • Macro signals (FRED API): Gas prices, U of M consumer sentiment index, unemployment — all freely available weekly/monthly via FRED. Connor asked for this explicitly. Adding 3–5 macro features to LightGBM is one sprint of feature engineering work. Seasonality already partially captures this; macro features improve the residual.
  • Circana integration: Covers Costco and a few others not in SPINS. Circana data is structurally similar to SPINS. Ingest pipeline would follow the same pattern as SPINS.
  • New distribution calendar: Sales team knows future store expansion dates. Even a simple CSV input of "SKU × retailer × stores × launch date" lets the model project forward-looking TDP ramp — currently completely missing from Aevah's forward forecast.
  • Competitor promo calendar (SPINS-derived): We already detect competitor promotions from SPINS base vs. incr units signals. Surfacing this as a feature in the promo lift model directly addresses Brian's "was a competitor on promo the same week?" variable.
4
For everything Connor does, how does Aevah make it better?

Walk through Connor's monthly cycle step by step:

  • Pull SPINS data (copy-paste 50K rows): → Aevah ingests automatically. Connor queries what he needs.
  • Update seasonality curve: → Aevah's compute_monthly_demand_index() refreshes automatically each cycle. Connor references a static output.
  • Open 100 sales planner files, extract promo data: → Replace with a structured promo input (shared sheet or Mo interface). Reps enter once; Connor and Aevah both consume.
  • Set base velocity per retailer-SKU: → Aevah extracts L12-week + LightGBM q50. Connor's override column still exists; Aevah's number is the starting point, not the override.
  • Apply promo lift estimates: → Aevah provides historical lift rates per retailer-SKU-promo-type as the default. Sales team overrides where they have additional context.
  • Estimate cannibalization: → Aevah's scored_cannibalization table provides the coefficient. Connor's model multiplies it in.
  • Handle new SKU / no-data retailers: → Proxy ramp from comparable BUILT launches (just demoed). For data-dark retailers, proxy from channel-matched SPINS retailers with confidence notation.
  • Build Monday flash: → Standard Aevah report: Mo forecast vs. Connor's forecast vs. MTD NetSuite. Pre-built, auto-updated, consistent format. Jeff and the sales team can review async; the Monday meeting becomes exception-driven rather than assembly-driven.
  • Ask "what will this promotion do?": → Mo Chat natural language: "Winco, CDc single, 2-for-$6, July. What lift?" — grounded in historical causal data, not a hallucination.

5 · The Supply Chain ROI Case

Connor said it plainly: "It informs the production team how much to make the month prior. And then that informs the procurement team how much ingredients they need to source, how much packaging they need to source." The forecast doesn't just affect revenue — it ripples through the entire operating model. Forecast error is not a reporting inconvenience; it's a cost driver.

Overforecast cost
Excess production → waste → spoilage. Connor has spoilage listed as a gross-to-net deduction. Overordering ingredients means cash tied up and potentially written off. Line staffing costs are committed in advance.
Underforecast cost
Stockouts → lost sales → retailer relationship risk. In a hyper-growth environment, being out of stock at a major account is disproportionately damaging — especially when a competitor fills the shelf space.
Promo planning lead time
Production is planned the month before the selling month. A wrong promo lift assumption isn't correctable in-month — the production decision is already locked. Better lift predictions = better inventory positioning ahead of promotional events.
Scale amplification
100 retailers × 156 SKUs × monthly. Even a 5% accuracy improvement across the portfolio compounds into significant P&L impact. At BUILT's growth rate, fixing the process now prevents this from becoming a structural problem at 200 retailers.
Key framing for Bracken
Aevah doesn't replace Connor's model. It improves the inputs to Connor's model and provides an independent second opinion. Connor keeps oversight, override control, and judgment. The ROI is: less time assembling data, better lift assumptions, earlier signal on distribution changes, and a forecast that doesn't degrade as the business scales from 100 to 200+ retailers.
