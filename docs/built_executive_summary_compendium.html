The Executive Answer

Mo gives you a more reliable view of future demand and a clearer explanation of what is driving it. That matters because BUILT's planning risk is not just "how many bars will we sell?" It is whether those units are coming from real demand, more doors, price cuts, promotions, or demand pulled from another BUILT SKU.

So What?

You get a better operating number and a practical range around it, instead of a single fragile forecast.

Why?

BUILT is a growth-mode brand. Raw unit trends can overstate demand when distribution is expanding.

Who Uses It?

Your finance, supply chain, sales, and trade teams all make costly commitments from the demand plan.

Bottom line: Mo functions as a finance control layer for demand planning, rather than a data science project. The value is fewer surprises, better trade spend calls, and a forecast that leadership can interrogate and trust.

What Changes for Your Finance Team

1. Revenue Planning Gets a Better Number

Instead of relying on a spreadsheet trend, you get a plan number grounded in recent sales, retailer behavior, distribution, pricing, and promotion signals.

2. Inventory Risk Gets a Range

Supply chain can plan against a floor, a plan, and an upside case. That makes safety stock, production, and purchasing decisions more defensible.

3. Trade Spend Gets Accountability

You can ask whether a promotion created incremental units or simply bought volume that would have happened anyway.

4. Growth Quality Becomes Visible

Mo separates "we sold more because we are in more stores" from "we sold more per store." Those are very different operating stories.

5. New SKU Launches Get Guardrails

New products with limited history are treated differently until they have enough sales pattern to support a more advanced forecast.

6. Forecast Meetings Get Explainable

Every meaningful change can be explained in plain English: distribution moved, price moved, recent velocity changed, or promo activity lifted units.

Pricing and Promotion: What It Means for Your Team

The Kroger Brownie Batter 4pk price move is the clearest example of why this matters to your team. A simple before-and-after report would say units went up. Mo explains how much likely came from the price cut, how much came from broader promotion support, and what that means for your future trade spend.

Question Plain-English Answer What It Means for You
What happened? Kroger's Brownie Batter 4pk price dropped from $10.99 to $10.14 in December 2025. You can evaluate a real pricing action, not a hypothetical.
Did units lift? Yes. Units ran about 29% above the estimated no-change baseline over the measured period. You can see the event created meaningful incremental volume.
Was it all due to price? No. The price move alone explains only part of the lift; display and feature support did the rest. You can judge the full promo package by whether the combined lift clears ROI thresholds.
What changes going forward? Each retailer has its own price-response profile. You can avoid generic price response assumptions that obscure account-level risk.
