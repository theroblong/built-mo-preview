The Executive Answer

Mo gives finance a more reliable view of future demand and a clearer explanation of what is driving it. That matters because BUILT's planning risk is not just "how many bars will we sell?" It is whether those units are coming from real demand, more doors, price cuts, promotions, or demand pulled from another BUILT SKU.

So What?

Finance gets a better operating number and a practical range around it, instead of a single fragile forecast.

Why?

BUILT is a growth-mode brand. Raw unit trends can overstate demand when distribution is expanding.

Who Cares?

Finance, supply chain, sales, and trade teams all make costly decisions from the demand plan.

Decision Needed

Approve a 30-45 day finance validation sprint using Connor's actual forecast history.

Bottom line: Mo should be positioned as a finance control layer for demand planning, not as a data science project. The value is fewer surprises, better trade spend calls, and a forecast that leadership can interrogate and trust.

What Changes for Finance

1. Revenue Planning Gets a Better Number

Instead of relying on a spreadsheet trend, finance gets a plan number grounded in recent sales, retailer behavior, distribution, pricing, and promotion signals.

2. Inventory Risk Gets a Range

Supply chain can plan against a floor, a plan, and an upside case. That makes safety stock, production, and purchasing decisions more defensible.

3. Trade Spend Gets Accountability

Finance can ask whether a promotion created incremental units or simply bought volume that would have happened anyway.

4. Growth Quality Becomes Visible

Mo separates "we sold more because we are in more stores" from "we sold more per store." Those are very different finance stories.

5. New SKU Launches Get Guardrails

New products with limited history are treated differently until they have enough sales pattern to support a more advanced forecast.

6. Forecast Meetings Get Explainable

Every meaningful change can be explained in plain English: distribution moved, price moved, recent velocity changed, or promo activity lifted units.

Pricing and Promotion: The Most CFO-Relevant Example

The Kroger Brownie Batter 4pk price move is the clearest "why finance should care" example. A simple before-and-after report would say units went up. Mo explains how much likely came from the price cut, how much came from broader promotion support, and what that means for future trade spend.

Question Plain-English Answer Finance Takeaway
What happened? Kroger's Brownie Batter 4pk price dropped from $10.99 to $10.14 in December 2025. Finance can evaluate a real pricing action, not a hypothetical.
Did units lift? Yes. Units ran about 29% above the estimated no-change baseline over the measured period. The event created meaningful incremental volume.
Was it all due to price? No. The price move alone explains only part of the lift; display and feature support did the rest. Finance should fund the full promo package only when the combined lift clears ROI thresholds.
What changes going forward? Each retailer should have its own price-response assumption. Promo business cases should stop using one generic price response across accounts.
