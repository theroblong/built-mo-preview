Executive Thesis

The three documents tell one coherent story: Mo turns BUILT's SPINS data into a finance-grade demand intelligence layer. It improves forecast accuracy, explains why demand is moving, and gives FP&A a defensible way to separate base demand, distribution-led growth, price response, promotion lift, and cannibalization risk.

Decision implication: Mo is no longer just an analytics demo. The evidence supports a finance validation sprint against Connor's actual historical forecasts, followed by integration into quarterly planning, trade spend review, and supply-chain demand commits.

What the Compendium Proves

1. Forecast Accuracy Is Materially Better

The best Q1 2026 validation lands at 4.3-4.4% wMAPE across 164 series, versus 24.6-27.5% for the strongest moving-average baselines and roughly 35% for manual CPG benchmarks.

2. The Model Is Auditable

SHAP waterfalls and driver decomposition expose the forecast logic in business terms: demand history, TDP trajectory, velocity, price, seasonality, and cannibalization.

3. It Answers Planning Questions

Finance gets floor, plan, and ceiling forecasts; sales gets account-level drivers; trade gets price and promo response; supply chain gets a quantitative operating range.

4. Distribution Is the Central Growth Variable

BUILT's growth-mode problem is that unit gains often come from more doors, not higher velocity. Mo separates TDP expansion from true per-store demand.

5. Trade Spend Can Be Causal

The Kroger Brownie Batter 4pk case isolates a Dec 2025 price event and estimates incremental lift rather than relying on raw before-and-after unit changes.

6. Retraining Protects the Value

A stale model degrades as the portfolio evolves. Quarterly retraining keeps the model aligned with new SKUs, distribution changes, and shifting retailer behavior.

Operating Decisions Enabled

  1. Revenue planning: Use q50 as the operating plan while tracking q10/q90 as downside and upside cases.
  2. Inventory and safety stock: Replace generic buffers with statistically validated floor and ceiling bands.
  3. Trade spend ROI: Separate base price response from display and feature lift before funding future promos.
  4. Distribution planning: Identify when growth is driven by TDP expansion versus durable velocity improvement.
  1. SKU launch governance: Route new SKUs through a cold-start model until enough history exists for LightGBM.
  2. Account prioritization: Use retailer-level velocity, TDP, and forecast bands to prioritize expansion bets.
  3. Quarterly planning cadence: Retrain after each SPINS refresh and publish accuracy by SKU and account.
  4. Finance narrative: Give the leadership team a plain-English reason for every meaningful forecast change.

Known Limits and Required Inputs

The model is strongest when it has current demand, distribution, and price signals. The main risks are forward-looking business events that SPINS only reveals after the fact.

Risk or Missing Input Why It Matters What BUILT Should Provide
Actual Excel baseline Confirms the real dollar value gap versus Connor's current process. Historical forecasts and actuals for priority SKU x retailer pairs.
Promo calendar Promo weeks are currently inferred retroactively from SPINS. H2 2026 retailer, SKU, week, TPR, display, and feature calendar.
Planogram and reset events TDP changes appear in SPINS with a 4-8 week lag. Known distribution wins, resets, new-door launches, and shelf changes.
Sell-in shipment data SPINS is sell-through; shipments reveal retailer ordering and supply risk earlier. Weekly shipments to retailer DCs by SKU and account.
Competitor pricing Competitor cuts can change velocity before BUILT sees it in own sales. Competitor ARP and promo signals where available from SPINS or internal tracking.
